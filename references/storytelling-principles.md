## Audience Analysis

### Know Your Reader Before You Write
Before rewriting anything, understand who will read it:
- What do they care about? What drives them?
- What scares them? What motivates them?
- What prevents them from acting?
- How do they measure success?
- What biases or constraints do they have?
- What is their opinion of you? Are you credible to them?

### Narrow Your Audience
When "everyone" is the audience, no one is. Narrow:
1. **Narrow to now**: who MUST receive this right now?
2. **Identify the decision maker**: who actually acts on this?
3. **Design for them**: then adapt for others if needed

### Mixed Audiences
When writing for diverse readers:
- Acknowledge different perspectives upfront
- Find common ground
- Signal when a section is specifically for a subgroup ("For the engineering team specifically...")
- Layer information: lead with what everyone needs, detail in appendices or follow-ups

## Message Framing

### The 3-Minute Story

If the reader had only 3 minutes, what would you tell them? Structure it as:

1. **Plot:** The context your reader needs to be ready for what's coming
2. **Twist:** The new, interesting, or unexpected information
3. **Ending:** The action you want them to take

This structure works for any length. An email, a report, or a single Slack message. The proportions change, but the sequence holds.

### What's at Stake

Frame the message around consequences that matter to the reader:
- **Positive framing:** What they stand to gain ("This recovers $300K")
- **Negative framing:** What they stand to lose ("We're losing $300K every quarter we don't act")

Choose based on audience and situation. When in doubt, try both and pick the one that creates more urgency.

### The Worst-Case Scenario Exercise
When the stakes aren't obvious, play it out in both directions:
- What happens if we do nothing? (worst case)
- What happens if we act? (best case)
- Where do the true stakes lie between these extremes?

## Story Structure

### Why Stories Beat Linear Presentations

The typical business communication follows a linear path: background -> methodology -> findings -> recommendations. This mirrors the writer's process, not the reader's needs. It's chronological, logical, and forgettable.

Stories have shape. They create tension, build to a peak, and resolve. This shape gives readers anchors. Things to remember and retell.

The shift from linear to story doesn't mean adding fiction. It means restructuring the same content so the reader feels the stakes and wants to keep reading.

### The Narrative Arc

1. **Plot (Setup):** What context does the reader need?
2. **Rising Action:** What complications or tensions exist? What's the problem getting worse?
3. **Climax:** The thing the reader most needs to understand
4. **Falling Action:** Additional details, options, potential solutions
5. **Resolution/Ending:** What should the reader do? The call to action.

### Identifying Tension

Tension is not drama. It's illuminating what's at stake.

Ask: What is the thing-gone-wrong or the thing-that-could-go-wrong? The tension should resonate with your reader, not just with you.

In a data update: the tension might be a trend that's heading the wrong way.
In a proposal: the tension is the cost of inaction.
In a project update: the tension is the risk or blocker.

Lead with this tension (don't bury it). It's what makes the reader care enough to keep reading.

### Linear vs. Story: An Example

**Linear (forgettable):**
"We conducted a survey of 500 customers. The methodology involved... We found that NPS increased. We also found customers splitting into camps. Our recommendation is..."

**Story (memorable):**
"Our customers are splitting into two camps. More fans, but also more unhappy customers, and fewer in the middle. NPS looks healthy on the surface, but underneath, the split is growing. We need to act before we start losing the unhappy ones. Here's what we recommend..."

Same facts. Different structure. The second version leads with the tension and makes the reader care immediately.

## Words & Titles

### Takeaway Titles (Not Topic Titles)

Every heading, subject line, or slide title should state the **conclusion**, not the **topic**.

| Topic Title (Weak) | Takeaway Title (Strong) |
|---|---|
| Supplier Analysis | Changing our supplier strategy could reduce costs |
| Competition Update | We're winning against every major competitor |
| Quarterly Business Review | (Mostly) strong results last quarter |
| Net Promoter Score | NPS up, but customers are splitting into camps |
| Data Storytelling | Bringing data to life through pictures and story |

### The Horizontal Logic Test
Read only the headers/titles in sequence. Do they tell the complete story? If someone read nothing but the headers, would they get the key message? If not, rewrite the headers.

### Strategic Text Placement
Along the narrative arc, text serves different purposes:
- **Setup sections:** Sparse text, just enough context
- **Middle sections:** More detail, revealed piece by piece
- **Closing sections:** Reiterate the takeaway or call to action

### Visual Hierarchy in Text
Not all text should be treated equally. Use:
- **Size, bold, color** to highlight what matters
- **Gray, smaller text** to de-emphasize supporting details
- **White space** to let important points breathe

## Data Communication

### Lead with the Insight, Not the Data

The data supports the story, not the other way around. Don't present numbers and hope the reader draws the right conclusion. State the conclusion, then use the data as evidence.

**Data-first (weak):** "Q3 revenue was $4.2M, up from $3.8M in Q2 and $3.1M in Q1."
**Insight-first (strong):** "Revenue is accelerating: $4.2M in Q3, up 35% since Q1. If this trend holds, we'll exceed our annual target by December."

### Direct Attention
When presenting numbers, tell the reader where to look and what to conclude. Don't make them do the math or figure out the pattern themselves.

### Avoid Chart Junk
In data-heavy rewrites, strip anything that doesn't serve the insight: redundant labels, decorative elements, unnecessary precision. The data should illuminate, not overwhelm.
